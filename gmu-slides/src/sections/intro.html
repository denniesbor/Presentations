Problem Statement: Disciplinary Silos

Core Problem: Studies remain confined within disciplinary boundaries without integrated treatment of uncertainty or cascading effects

Space Physics

Solar-terrestrial models, magnetosphere dynamics

No infrastructure coupling

Engineering

Power system modeling, component fragility

No economic propagation

Economics

High-level risk estimates, scenario-based

No physical grounding

Pulkkinen et al., 2008; Marshalko et al., 2023

Problem Statement: Current Limitations

Existing Approaches

  • Isolate infrastructures, rely on deterministic scenarios
  • Neglect uncertainty propagation and inter-sectoral dependencies
  • Treat state-level grids as electrically independent
  • Lack physically grounded hazard-fragility-economics linkages

Oughton et al., 2017; Schulte et al., 2014

Assessment Challenges

  • Power: Transformer-specific vulnerability patterns from spatially varying GIC
  • Satellites: Orbital service areas, spectrum licensing, MHD/plasma models
  • Economics: Deterministic loss estimates without macroeconomic propagation

This Work: Unified stochastic framework with end-to-end uncertainty propagation from space weather forcing to national-scale economic losses

Research Question 1

Physical Vulnerability

How does extreme space weather physically stress critical infrastructure systems, and what are the quantified uncertainties in those stress estimates?

Power Grid

  • GIC loading, transformer overheating probability, spatial clustering of high-risk substations
  • Monte Carlo propagation: network topology, transformer aging, grounding configurations

Orbital Assets

  • Drag-driven orbital decay, propellant usage, radiation-driven anomaly probability
  • Loss-of-service risk in LEO, MEO, GEO
  • UQ over atmospheric density models, orbital parameters, radiation transport

Research Question 2

Socio-Economic Consequence

How do those physical stresses and their uncertainties translate into quantifiable service disruption, affected population, and daily economic loss?

Analysis Chain

Infrastructure degradation → Direct loss of delivered service → Trace through interdependent sectors (IO/CGE models) → Probabilistic loss distributions

Outputs

  • Affected population with spatial distribution
  • Sectoral output loss with uncertainty bounds
  • Economy-wide daily GDP impact with confidence intervals
  • Not deterministic point estimates — full probability distributions

Research Question 3

Comparative Risk Assessment

How does space weather risk compare to more commonly experienced natural hazards in terms of physical stress, economic impact, and affected infrastructure?

Approach

Apply same physics-to-economics framework to:

  • Space weather (geomagnetic storms, particle flux)
  • Tropical cyclones (wind, surge)
  • Floods (inundation depth/extent)
  • Earthquakes (ground shaking)

Goal: First national-scale, apples-to-apples comparison of infrastructure vulnerability and economic exposure across hazards with integrated UQ

Enable statements like: "A 100-year geomagnetic storm affects X million people vs. Y million for Category 4 hurricane"

Research Objectives

Objective 1: Physics-to-Engineering Coupling

Quantify component stress, degradation, failure probability under extreme storms with explicit propagation of epistemic and aleatory uncertainties

GIC exposure, transformer thermal loading, orbital decay, Δv demand, radiation dose, SEE probability

Objective 2: Impact Propagation

Translate physical degradation into socio-economic impact with probabilistic loss distributions

Service territories → affected population → sectoral output → IO/CGE models → economy-wide daily GDP impact with confidence intervals

Objective 3: Cross-Hazard Assessment

Apply framework to multiple hazard classes with consistent UQ to produce directly comparable risk metrics

Space weather contextualized alongside hurricanes, floods, earthquakes
