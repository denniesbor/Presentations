Why Space Weather Matters

Space weather = variable conditions of the Sun, solar wind, and Earth's magnetosphere–ionosphere that can disrupt technological systems essential to modern society.

May 2024 "Gannon" storm: most severe geomagnetic disturbance since 2003
  • Infrequent but severe hazards
  • Probability increases during solar maximum (~11-year cycle)
  • March 1989 storm: Hydro-Québec system collapse, millions without power

Despite advances in space physics and engineering, quantitative frameworks linking geophysical forcing to economic loss remain limited

Problem Statement

Current approaches to space weather risk assessment:

  • Remain confined within disciplinary boundaries
  • Focus on either solar physics, power systems, or economic estimates
  • Lack physically grounded linkages between hazard, infrastructure, and impacts
  • Provide deterministic estimates without uncertainty quantification

This work addresses these limitations through a unified, stochastic framework that explicitly propagates uncertainty from space weather forcing through engineering system behavior to economic losses.

Research Questions

RQ1: Physical Vulnerability

How does extreme space weather physically stress critical infrastructure systems, and what are the quantified uncertainties?

RQ2: Socio-Economic Consequence

How do those physical stresses translate into service disruption, affected population, and economic loss?

RQ3: Comparative Risk Assessment

How does space weather risk compare to more commonly experienced natural hazards?

Research Objectives

  • Physics-to-Engineering Mapping: Develop coupled physics–engineering models that quantify component stress with explicit propagation of uncertainties
  • Impact Translation: Translate physical degradation into socio-economic impact with probabilistic loss distributions
  • Cross-Hazard Assessment: Apply the framework to multiple hazard classes with consistent uncertainty treatment

Significance & Contribution

  1. End-to-end coupling from solar forcing to economy-wide impact with preserved uncertainty
  2. Multi-system coverage spanning terrestrial and space-based infrastructure
  3. Operator-facing and policy-facing metrics (transformer failure probability, propellant consumption, affected customers, GDP impact)
  4. Cross-hazard comparative assessment positioning space weather alongside familiar hazards
  5. Open-source tooling with documented workflows for validation and adoption
