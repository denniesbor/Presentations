Publication I: Power System Vulnerability

Historical Context

  • March 1989: Hydro-Québec collapse, 9-hour blackout
  • May 2024 "Gannon": Widespread GIC across North America

Cander, 2019; Caraballo et al., 2025; Lawrence et al., 2025

Problem: Disconnect between physics and economics

  • Economic studies assume blackouts without physical basis
  • Physics studies don't extend to economic impacts
  • Earlier models treat state grids as independent

Schulte et al., 2014; Oughton et al., 2017

Research Questions & Objectives

Hypothesis

Large-scale GMDs can be translated into geographically resolved failure probabilities with quantified uncertainties, then propagated into macroeconomic loss distributions

RQ1: Vulnerability Assessment

Spatial distribution of transformer/substation failure risk across U.S. grid under extreme storms?

Probabilistic: "40% failure probability under 150-yr storm"

RQ2: Economic Impact Quantification

Per-day economic impact, population, business exposures from failures?

Direct loss + indirect sectoral propagation via IO/CGE

Technical Objectives

Compute geoelectric fields → GIC flows (Lehtinen-Pirjola) → failure probability (fragility + aging) → service territories → IO/CGE propagation

Methodology

Integrated framework

Methodology

~60,000 substations, 161–765 kV

1. Geomagnetic Model

INTERMAGNET/USGS/NRCan (1985–2024) → SECS interpolation → EarthScope MT → power-law extrapolation

2. GIC Calculation

OSM + EIA grid → Lehtinen–Pirjola: $(Y_e + Y_n)\,V_n = J_e$ → Monte Carlo (topology, grounding)

3. Reliability

Lognormal fragility: $P_{fail}=\Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln I_{GIC}-\ln 75}{\beta}\right)$, $\beta\sim U(0.25,0.50)$ + Weibull aging

4. Economics

Voronoi territories → dasymetric → Ghosh IO → GDP ($/day$) with CIs

Results: Hazard Characterization

Historical: March 1989 (2071 nT, 22 V/km), May 2024 "Gannon" (1870 nT, 12 V/km)
Extreme: 100-yr (25 V/km) → 250-yr (31 V/km)

Findings

  • Geoelectric maxima in Upper Midwest & Atlantic seaboard
  • Subsurface conductivity amplifies coupling
  • Return-period scaling preserves spatial pattern; magnitudes rise
  • 1989 < 2003 < 2024 baseline < 100–250-yr scenarios

Results: GIC Exposure & Failures

GIC hotspots: 200–400 A/phase (250-yr)
Transformer failure probabilities: >40% at isolated substations

Takeaways

  • Hotspots align with conductive subsurface & dense HV topology
  • Higher return period ⇒ broader 200–400 A/phase spread
  • Failure risk concentrates in Northeast & Upper Midwest

Results: Economic Impact

Direct + indirect GDP by sector (Ghosh IO)

Economic Impacts (per day)

  • 100-yr: $1.4 ± 0.2 B/day
  • 150-yr: $1.7 ± 0.2 B/day
  • 250-yr: $2.1 ± 0.3 B/day

Finance, real estate, professional services dominate; indirect adds ~50%.

Validation Metric

Prediction efficiency (PE): $PE = 1 - \frac{\sum(y_{pred}- y_{obs})^2}{\sum(y_{obs}-\bar{y})^2}$

PE near 1.0 = perfect; negative = worse than mean

Contributions & Expected Impact

Key Contributions

  1. Physics-to-infrastructure coupling with UQ: Continental-scale, Monte Carlo across all stages
  2. Spatially explicit probabilistic mapping: Asset-level failure probabilities (not generic regions)
  3. End-to-end uncertainty propagation: Physics → economics with confidence intervals
  4. Policy relevance + open-source tools: NERC GMD standards, reproducible workflows

Expected Impact

  • Risk-informed grid hardening (target high-failure substations)
