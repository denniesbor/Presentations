Methodology

Integrated framework

Methodology

~60,000 substations, 161–765 kV

1. Geomagnetic Model

INTERMAGNET/USGS/NRCan (1985–2024) → SECS interpolation → EarthScope MT → power-law extrapolation

2. GIC Calculation

OSM + EIA grid → Lehtinen–Pirjola: $(Y_e + Y_n)\,V_n = J_e$ → Monte Carlo (topology, grounding)

3. Reliability

Lognormal fragility: $P_{fail}=\Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln I_{GIC}-\ln 75}{\beta}\right)$, $\beta\sim U(0.25,0.50)$ + Weibull aging

4. Economics

Voronoi territories → dasymetric → Ghosh IO → GDP ($/day$) with CIs

Results: Hazard Characterization

Historical: March 1989 (2071 nT, 22 V/km), May 2024 "Gannon" (1870 nT, 12 V/km)
Extreme: 100-yr (25 V/km) → 250-yr (31 V/km)

Findings

  • Geoelectric maxima in Upper Midwest & Atlantic seaboard
  • Subsurface conductivity amplifies coupling
  • Return-period scaling preserves spatial pattern; magnitudes rise
  • 1989 < 2003 < 2024 baseline < 100–250-yr scenarios

Results: GIC Exposure & Failures

GIC hotspots: 200–400 A/phase (250-yr)
Transformer failure probabilities: >40% at isolated substations

Takeaways

  • Hotspots align with conductive subsurface & dense HV topology
  • Higher return period ⇒ broader 200–400 A/phase spread
  • Failure risk concentrates in Northeast & Upper Midwest

Validation Metric

Prediction efficiency (PE): $PE = 1 - \frac{\sum(y_{pred}- y_{obs})^2}{\sum(y_{obs}-\bar{y})^2}$
