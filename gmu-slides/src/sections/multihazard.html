Publication III: Multi-Hazard Comparative Assessment

Motivation

Space weather risk assessment remains embryonic compared to terrestrial hazards

  • Studied in isolation
  • Policymakers cannot compare to hurricanes, floods, earthquakes
  • Single-hazard approaches miss compound/cascading effects

Hochrainer-Stigler et al., 2023; Saruniene et al., 2024; Rokhideh et al., 2025

Problem: Lack of unified framework for cross-hazard comparison

  • Existing multi-hazard tools use semi-quantitative scores
  • No physical grounding or uncertainty treatment
  • Cannot produce "apples-to-apples" risk metrics

This Work: Apply same physics-to-economics framework to space weather, hurricanes, floods, earthquakes → directly comparable metrics

Research Questions & Objectives

Hypothesis

A unified framework can produce comparable metrics across space weather, tropical cyclones, floods, earthquakes through consistent treatment of forcing, exposure, response, and propagation with explicit UQ

RQ1: Methodological Integration

How to harmonize various hazard methodologies while preserving hazard-specific physics?

RQ2: Comparative Risk Assessment

How do space weather impacts compare quantitatively to hurricanes, floods, earthquakes?

For example: "100-yr GMD ≈ Category X hurricane in region Y"

RQ3: Cross-Hazard Interdependencies

How do hazard interactions and infrastructure interdependencies amplify/attenuate impacts?

Expected Results: Comparative Risk Metrics

Economic Impacts (100-yr events)

  • Space Weather: ~$1.4B/day (power grid)
  • Hurricane (Cat 3): $0.9–2.1B/day (regional)
  • Earthquake (New Madrid): $3.5–4.0B/day
  • Major Flood: $0.8–1.8B/day (basin-dependent)

Direct comparison enables contextualization for stakeholders

Geographic Differentiation

  • Space Weather: Northern latitudes (Northeast, Midwest, PNW)
  • Hurricanes: Gulf & Atlantic coasts
  • Earthquakes: Major fault systems
  • Floods: River basins, coastal zones

Infrastructure Vulnerability (by hazard)

  • Power: 5–40% (space weather), 10–30% (hurricane), 15–35% (earthquake)
  • Transport: <5% (space weather), 15–35% (flood/earthquake)
  • Telecom: Complex dependencies across all hazards

Recovery Timeframes

  • Space Weather: 12–72 hours (grid); months (satellites)
  • Hurricane/Flood: 2–4 weeks
  • Earthquake: 1–6 months (severe areas)

Key Finding: Space weather comparable to regional Cat 3 hurricane but with distinct geographic footprint and recovery profile

Contributions & Expected Impact

Key Contributions

  1. Unified Multi-Hazard Framework: First truly integrated methodology bridging space weather & terrestrial hazards with preserved physics
  2. Quantitative Contextualization: Position space weather within natural hazard landscape using familiar metrics
  3. Cross-Domain Interdependency Modeling: Explicit capture of compound events & cascading failures
  4. National-Scale Comparative Metrics: "Apples-to-apples" comparison with consistent UQ

Expected Impact

  • Emergency managers understand space weather vs hurricanes
  • Policymakers prioritize resilience investments
  • Insurers incorporate space weather into portfolios
  • Regional multi-hazard exposure profiles

Policy Alignment

  • Sendai Framework: compound/cascading hazards
  • National Space Weather Strategy: comparative risk
  • UNDRR: national resilience metrics
